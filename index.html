Aston Dynamics Canada · Sep 2025 – Present

Making the factory smarter

After 16 months as an intern, Aston Dynamics asked me to stay on as an independent consultant — which meant taking on more open-ended problems with less hand-holding. The first big one: the factory was catching brake actuator defects too late in the process, and test cycles were slow and inconsistent.

I designed a dedicated test rig from the ground up. The goal was automated detection of manufacturing and assembly defects — the kind of issues that are cheap to catch early and expensive to miss. The rig cut overall test cycle time by 30% while keeping full defect coverage. It's now part of the production line.

I also ran a material evaluation on thermal adhesives for one of their electronics assemblies. After a structured comparative study, I recommended a replacement that improved thermal conductivity by 65% — without compromising any mechanical or manufacturing constraints.

"The interesting problems aren't the ones where you already know the answer. They're the ones where the constraints are real and the tradeoffs actually matter."

Beyond my own deliverables, I've been running design reviews for new products and working with interns on component selection and manufacturability.

Aston Dynamics Canada · May 2024 – Sep 2025

From breadboard to 200 km on the road

My internship started with a genuinely hard brief: design the company's first brake controller with GPS, Bluetooth, and CAN communication all on a single board. There was no prior design to iterate on — just a set of requirements and a blank schematic.

Through iterative prototyping, I landed on a design that reduced the PCB footprint by 60% and improved power efficiency by 23%. The board needed to handle 30A continuous output, which meant thermal modelling wasn't optional — I validated that through E-load testing before the design went further. The result opened up two new product lines.

Alongside the hardware work, I ran a full DFMEA for a new product line — identifying 12 critical failure modes and building in mitigation strategies to achieve fail-safe operation. Then came field testing: 200 km across multiple tow platforms, verifying electrical reliability and communication stability in conditions that don't show up on a bench.

I also took a pass at the bill of materials across several product lines and found 12% in cost savings through consolidation and smarter part selection — without touching any performance specs.

UofT Formula SAE (UTFR) · Vehicle Controls Lead · May 2025 – Present

Rebuilding the car's nervous system

As Vehicle Controls Lead, I inherited a steer-by-wire system that worked — but not well enough. The chain-driven architecture introduced backlash that undermined autonomous steering precision. The fix wasn't a tweak; it was a full redesign. I moved to direct actuator integration with a custom steering rack, eliminating the backlash at the source.

The pneumatic emergency braking system had a similar problem: it ran out of stored energy after just 3 brake maneuvers per charge. After rethinking the circuit layout, I redesigned the pneumatic architecture and got that number to 34 maneuvers per charge.

On the modelling side, I extended the team's steady-state brake rotor thermal model into a transient Simulink simulation — showing how temperature and friction coefficient evolve during a competition run, validated to within 5% of measured data. Across both subsystems, targeted design optimization delivered a 2.5 kg mass reduction.

UofT Formula SAE (UTFR) · Brakes Lead · May 2023 – May 2024

Building the team's first autonomous brake system from zero

When I joined UTFR as Brakes Lead, the team didn't have a working autonomous emergency brake system. Driverless competition categories were coming, and without one, we couldn't run. So we built it.

The first functional prototype achieved a 38% performance improvement over earlier benchtop attempts. Getting there required measuring brake system compliance — volumetric expansion of brake lines under pressure — which informed material choices that reduced system compliance by 44%.

I built a steady-state thermal model, ran coupled structural-thermal FEA in ANSYS, and validated predictions to within 9% of measured data.

Leading a team of 8 to build something genuinely new — and watching it work on an actual car — was one of the most satisfying engineering experiences I've had.

That year, the team finished 1st at the Formula Hybrid+Electric competition and was selected for the Design Finals event at FSAE Michigan.
