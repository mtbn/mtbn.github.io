UofT Formula SAE (UTFR) · Brakes Lead · May 2023 – May 2024

Building the team's first autonomous brake system from zero

When I joined UTFR as Brakes Lead, the team didn't have a working autonomous emergency brake system. Driverless competition categories were coming, and without one, we couldn't run. So we built it.

The first functional prototype achieved a 38% performance improvement over earlier benchtop attempts. Getting there required actually measuring brake system compliance by pressurizing lines and recording volumetric expansion. That data informed material selection and brought system compliance down by 44%.

I built a steady-state thermal model, ran coupled structural-thermal FEA in ANSYS to check for thermal stress, and validated predictions to within 9% of measured data. At steady state the rotor cycles between 320 and 400°C. Structurally it holds a minimum safety factor of 3.33.

Leading a team of 8 to build something genuinely new, and watching it work on an actual car, was one of the most satisfying engineering experiences I've had.

That year, the team finished 1st at Formula Hybrid+Electric and was selected for the Design Finals at FSAE Michigan. The six designs below cover the component-level work from that year.

Hydraulic-over-pneumatic EBS piston

The challenge with a pneumatic emergency brake is getting enough line pressure out of a small stored air supply. This design uses a differential piston: a large pneumatic bore drives a smaller hydraulic bore, multiplying the force. When the solenoid valves open on power loss, compressed air drives the piston and pushes fluid into the brake lines. Bore sizes were calculated from the required line pressure and master cylinder displacement. O-rings were spec'd to Parker guidelines. It delivers 50% more braking force from the same air supply as the previous generation.

Autonomous System Brake V1

The ASB V1 was the team's first brake-by-wire prototype, co-designed with the previous Brakes Lead. The intent was to get something on the car quickly for driverless testing without modifying the existing manual brake circuit. A linear actuator drives the master cylinder; a pneumatic cylinder provides emergency backup. The whole thing mounts as a bolt-on unit with quick-disconnect fittings. It was never formally run in competition, but a lot of what we figured out building it carried forward into the current system.

Brake rotor thermal and structural analysis

Heat flux and convection coefficients came from first-principles calculations, which fed into an ANSYS thermal simulation. Those thermal results then fed into a structural simulation to check for stress from thermal expansion. At steady state the rotor cycles between 320 and 400°C after about 50 hard stops from 70 km/h at 1.4g. Structurally, the minimum safety factor is 3.33, with stress concentrations at the inner geometry transitions where the mounting cutouts create sharp corners. Thermal predictions came within 9% of thermocouple data on the car.

Adjustable pedal tray and rail

Adjustable pedals are a requirement since drivers vary a lot in size. The OEM rail system had too much compliance, and it was showing up in the brake pedal feel. The fix was a fully custom tray and rail machined to tighter tolerances. The ANSYS deformation result shows the load path clearly: the custom rail stays essentially rigid, and the deformation concentrates at the central brackets directly under the load. Under maximum brake load, the new design deflects 37% less than the prior setup.

Throttle pedal redesign

Driver comfort was the starting point. The previous throttle pedal sat too steep, putting the driver's foot in an uncomfortable position. Lowering the pedal angle meant rethinking the return spring, since the geometry no longer worked for a torsion spring. The replacement is a compression spring on a guided rod with heim joints at each end. That change also required swapping the linear position sensor for a rotary potentiometer at the pivot, since the pedal motion is purely rotational about that axis.

Brake pressure and deceleration model

The question was: for any combination of front and rear brake line pressures, what deceleration does the car achieve? The Python program sweeps a grid of pressure values and computes the result for each one using a model of the car's brake geometry, wheel loads, and tire behavior. Any combination where the model assumptions break down gets filtered out. MATLAB renders the valid space as a 3D surface. The wedge shape tells you something real: deceleration scales with both pressures, but the valid region is bounded by wheel lockup on each axle, and the front bias is steeper than most people would guess.
